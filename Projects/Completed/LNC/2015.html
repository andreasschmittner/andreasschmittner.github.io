Accomplishments

* What are the major goals of the project?

The major goals are to test hypotheses that variations in tidal mixing due to the lunar nodal cycle (LNC) affect climate in the North Pacific.

* What was accomplished under these goals (you must provide information for at least one of the 4 categories below)?

Major Activities:

Postdoc David Ullman and PI Schmittner have worked with Gokhan Danabasoglu and Nancy Norton at NCAR to implement the subgrid-scale tidal mixing scheme (Schmittner and Egbert, 2014) into the ocean component (POP2) of the Community Earth System Model (CESM). The implementation, including separate treatments for diurnal and semi-diurnal tides, was successful and several sensitivity experiments with POP2 have been conducted.

Short experiments (1 month), starting from observed initial conditions, were used to compare model predicted diapycnal diffusivities between the different models and with observational estimates based on microstructure measurements, hydrography and ARGO floats. Long simulations have been conducted in order to test the sensitivity of the global meridional overturning circulation (MOC) to the consideration of subgrid-scale bathymetry, spatial distribution of tidal energy input, and regularization choices (limits on diffusivities) used in the parameterization. Results from the long simulations have been compared to the previous version of CESM and to CFC observations.

Collaborator Malte Mueller (Norwegian Meteorological Institute) has kindly provided time series the different tidal constituents. Those will be used for the time varying tidal mixing simulations. Nancy Norton has sucessfully implemented the time varying mixing in POP2.

In collaboration with Mattias Green and Sophie-Beatrice Wilmes (U. Bangor, UK) Schmittner has conducted simulations with the UVic model exploring the effects of different sea level during the Last Glacial Maximum (LGM) on tidal energy dissipation, deep ocean mixing and the MOC. These experiments were not originally planned, but they provide an opportunity to extend the investiation of time varying tidal mixing effects to longer time scales.

Specific Objectives:

The objectives of the ocean-only experiments are to (1) test the sensitivity of the model predicted spatial distribution of mixing (diffusivities) and its effects on the MOC and (2) to identify model versions that agree best with observations. Those models will be used subsequently in transient simulations with time varying tidal mixing.

Once the ocean-only experiments with time varying tidal mixing have been evaluated we will conduct fully coupled experiments with CESM with time varying tidal mixing.

The goal of the LGM experiments was to quantify the effect of increased deep ocean mixing due to sea level lowering on the MOC.

Significant Results:

Results from the short simulations show that considering subgrid-scale bathymetry shifts mixing upward in the water column, consistent with earlier results from the UVic model. (The effect is smaller than in the UVic model because POP2 resolves more of the bathymetry due to its higher resolution.) Nevertheless, these results suggest that the subgrid-scale scheme may still be useful in models of ~1 deg resolution.

Model-model comparisons show that new estimates of tidal energy input (by Egbert and Green) combined with subgrid-scale bathymetry and high regularization limits (kv < 100 cm2/s) lead to more mixing in the abyssal ocean, faster flow of circumpolar deep water into the Pacific and more northward penetration of bottom water into the North Pacific compared with the original scheme used in CESM (which used no subgrid-scale scheme and energy fields from Jayne). This change in the circulation will likely improve a known large bias of too old deep waters in the North Pacific in CESM. However, these simulations also introduce a drift towards colder deep ocean temperatures and they significantly affect mixed layer depths in the Southern Ocean. Comparisons to CFC observations indicates that the models with higher abyssal mixing tend to overestimate CFC penetration in the Southern Ocean. However, mixed layer depths are also influenced by other model parameters and thus this result does not imply necessarily that the high mixing models are unrealistic.

Comparison to diffusivity observations indicates that considering subgrid-scale bathymetry is generally slightly more realistic and that small values for the regularization limits (kv < 5-10 cm2/s) produces unrealistically small diffusivities in regions where high diffusivities have been observed such as the Kuril Straits. However, significant biases remain. E.g. diffusivity profiles predicted by all model versions are too steep in Hawaii, indicating that the vertical decay profile may not be right there. We are currently experimenting with a different vertical decay profile.

The LGM experiments show a large increase in deep ocean mixing due to the 125 m lower sea level. This causes in a large (30-80%) increase in the MOC, particularly the bottom flow is accelerated.

Key outcomes or Other achievements:

* What opportunities for training and professional development has the project provided?

Postdoctoral researcher David Ullman was trained in using POP2 and the UVic model. Ullman participated in various scientific meetings and in one professional development workshop (Aug 3-8, 2014, at William Mitchel College of Law, St. Paul, MN, http://web.wmitchell.edu/expert-witness-training-academy/). Ullman taught two classes in Schmittner's undergraduate course "The Changing Climate" (ATS 320).

* How have the results been disseminated to communities of interest?

Results have been presented at the Ocean Sciences Meeting.

* What do you plan to do during the next reporting period to accomplish the goals?

We will run ocean only experiments with time varying LNC tidal forcing and analyze the results.

We will run fully coupled CESM model simulations with time varying LNC forcing and analyze the results.

Results will be written up in at least one peer-reviewed publication.

Technologies or Techniques

  • Modification to the CESM code were implemented.The CESM code is freely available. Code modifications contributed in this product will also be made publicly available at the end of the project.

    Ullman wrote matlab scripts that calculate the three-dimensional distribution of tidal energy input fields to the climate based on two-dimensional energy flux maps from a tide model and a high resolution bathymetric dataset. These scripts will be made publicly available for CESM users.

Impacts

What is the impact on the development of the principal discipline(s) of the project?

The project will likely advance physical oceanography and climate research by evaluating if the 18.6 year precessional cycle of moon's orbit around the Earth could cause climate variations by modulating ocean mixing.

What is the impact on other disciplines?

Nothing to report.

What is the impact on the development of human resources?

The project contributed to the professional development of a postdoctoral scholar, Dr. David Ullman. Dr. Ullman developed research skills by learning how to use two climate models, compare model output to observations, and to interpret the results. He also developed teaching and communication skills by teaching undergraduate classes and by participating in a communications training workshop.

What is the impact on physical resources that form infrastructure?

Nothing to report.

What is the impact on institutional resources that form infrastructure?

Nothing to report.

What is the impact on information resources that form infrastructure?

The project has contributed to the improvement of a state-of-the-science numerical climate model, the National Center for Atmospheric Research's (NCAR) Community Earth System Climate Model (CESM). The CESM is used by a large community of scientists around the world and contributes to the major assessment reports from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC).

What is the impact on technology transfer?

The CESM code is publicly available and our contributions, that is the scheme of subgrid-scale bathymetry as well as scripts required to calculate input fields, will become freely and publicly available with a future CESM release.

What is the impact on society beyond science and technology?

Nothing to report.
